## 1. What oxidative stress IS (mechanism)

- Reactive oxygen species (ROS: superoxide O2•−, hydrogen peroxide H2O2, hydroxyl radical •OH) are normal by-products of metabolism. >90% of cellular ROS come from the **mitochondrial electron transport chain**; also endoplasmic reticulum, plasma membrane, peroxisomes. [A1, S1]
- The bird neutralizes ROS with an **antioxidant defense system**:
  - **Superoxide dismutase (SOD)** converts superoxide → H2O2.
  - **Catalase (CAT)** and **glutathione peroxidase (GPx)** break H2O2 → water. GPx is a **selenium-dependent** enzyme.
  - **Glutathione**, plus dietary antioxidants **vitamin E (α-tocopherol)**, **vitamin C**, and **selenium** (GPx cofactor). [A1, A2]
- **Oxidative stress = the balance tips**: ROS production overwhelms antioxidant defense. Excess ROS then attack:
  - **Membrane lipids → lipid peroxidation** (biomarker: **malondialdehyde, MDA**; end-product 4-hydroxynonenal damages membranes). [A1, A2]
  - **Proteins** (protein oxidation/nitration) and **DNA** (biomarker **8-OHdG**). [A1]
- Plain-language frame for farmers: the bird has a built-in "rust-control" system. Stress makes cells produce damaging molecules faster than the system can mop them up, so cells start getting damaged from the inside — like rust on metal.

## 2. CAUSES (on-farm triggers)

- **Heat stress** — high body temperature increases mitochondrial electron leak → ROS surge. The #1 studied cause. [A1, S1]
- **High stocking density** — independently raises lipid peroxidation and DNA/protein oxidation. [S2]
- **Oxidized / rancid fats and oils in feed** (high peroxide value) — feed itself delivers pre-formed oxidation products; reduces palatability and villus height. Guideline: fats/oils PV < 5 meq/kg. [S3]
- **Mycotoxins** (aflatoxin, deoxynivalenol, etc.) — hepatic oxidative stress, ↑MDA, ↓antioxidant enzymes. [S4]
- **Rapid growth rate of modern broilers** — high metabolic rate → higher baseline ROS load; modern broilers show more heat-stress gut leak than ancestral jungle fowl. [A1, S6]
- **Infection / immune activation** (coccidiosis, bacterial LPS), **transport, catching, thinning, poor air quality (ammonia)**. [A1, A2]

## 3. PATHOGENICITY (how it harms the bird)

- **Gut barrier breakdown ("leaky gut")**: ROS + heat reduce villus height and crypt depth and lower tight-junction proteins (claudin, occludin, ZO-1). The barrier leaks → bacteria/toxins translocate into blood → inflammation. [A2, S6]
- **Immunosuppression**: oxidative damage + leaky-gut inflammation weaken immune competence → the bird becomes MORE susceptible to secondary infection, and vaccine "reactions" can look worse. [A1, A2]  ← KEY link to the misattribution point.
- **Systemic cell damage**: membrane lipid peroxidation, protein and DNA damage across tissues. [A1]
- **Meat quality**: lipid oxidation → drip loss, rancidity, shorter shelf life (esp. from oxidized feed). [S3]
- **Breast-muscle myopathies (woody breast / white striping)** in fast-growing broilers are associated with localized hypoxia/oxidative stress — CITE ONLY AFTER verifying a specific myopathy-oxidative-stress source at write time (flagged, not yet verified).

## 4. EFFECT ON PERFORMANCE (the numbers the user asked about)

- **↓ Average daily feed intake (ADFI)** — birds eat less (heat suppresses appetite; oxidized feed is unpalatable). [S3, L1]
- **↓ Average daily gain (ADG) / body weight** — less intake + energy diverted to damage control + poorer nutrient absorption from damaged gut. [S3]
- **↑ Feed conversion ratio (FCR)** — worse feed efficiency per kg gain. [A1, S3]
- **↑ Mortality** — severe/acute heat stress kills; chronic oxidative damage plus secondary infection raises culls and deaths. [A1, L1]
- Net: this is exactly the "poor performance" pattern (bad FCR, low gain, higher mortality) that is often blamed on infection or the vaccine.

## 5. HOW TO REDUCE IT (mitigation) — management first, then nutrition

**Management (remove the trigger):**
- Control heat: ventilation and **air speed 1–3 m/s over broilers** (min 1 m/s; 0.7 m/s min for breeders/layers), evaporative cooling, insulation, cool drinking water, run ventilation overnight before a hot day. [L1]
- Reduce stocking density in hot weather; minimize transport/catching/thinning stress; good air quality (low ammonia). [L1, S2, A1]

**Feed quality:**
- Use fresh fats/oils (**PV < 5 meq/kg**), avoid rancid/old feed, store feed cool and dry, protect fats with feed antioxidants. [S3]
- Mycotoxin control: test feed, use binders/detoxifiers. [S4]

**Dietary antioxidants (support the defense system):**
- **Vitamin E + selenium (organic Se)** — synergistic; raise SOD/GPx, lower MDA under heat stress. [A1, S5]
- **Vitamin C**, **polyphenols/phytogenics** (resveratrol, thymol/carvacrol, luteolin, plant extracts), **betaine**, **L-glutamine**, **probiotics/prebiotics**. [A1, A2]
- Exact inclusion rates vary by product/strain/condition — must cite a specific verified source for any dose that goes into a course; do not state a mg/kg without a matching verified citation.

## 6. THE CORE MESSAGE (user's framing)

Oxidative stress is a leading **non-infectious** driver of poor broiler/breeder/layer performance
(poor FCR, low daily gain, higher mortality). It is routinely **misread as "an infection," "a bad
vaccine," or "the veterinarian's fault"** when the real root cause is heat, stocking density,
oxidized/moldy feed, or other management/nutritional stress that overwhelmed the bird's antioxidant
defenses and THEN opened the door to secondary infection or amplified vaccine reactions. The fix is
to find and correct the management/feed trigger and support the antioxidant defense system — not to
reach first for antibiotics or to blame the vaccine.

## 2026-07-20 Addendum — Energy partitioning hierarchy + acute phase proteins

Added to explain WHY oxidative stress hits performance first. Two new anchors verified this session:

- **[A3] Lopez G, Leeson S. Review: Energy partitioning in broiler chickens. Can J Anim Sci. 2008;88(2):205-212. doi:10.4141/cjas07087.** Verified via CrossRef 2026-07-20 (title, both authors, journal, year, vol/issue/pages, DOI). Anchor for the maintenance-first / production-last energy hierarchy.
- **[A4] Barnes DM, Song Z, Klasing KC, Bottje W. Protein metabolism during an acute phase response in chickens. Amino Acids. 2002;22(1):15-26. doi:10.1007/s726-002-8198-6.** Verified via PubMed (PMID 12025871) + CrossRef 2026-07-20 (DOI, vol/issue/pages). After LPS immune challenge: liver protein synthesis +141%, plasma protein +161%, hemopexin +266%; tissue free amino acids dropped 16-41%. Anchor for acute phase proteins diverting amino acids/energy from growth.

**The energy priority ladder (teaching model, grounded in [A3]):** a bird pays its energy "bills" in
order: (1) basal metabolism, (2) thermoregulation, (3) feather cover, (4) activity, (5) body weight
gain, (6) egg production. Maintenance (1-4) is met first; production (5-6) is funded only with what is
left. NOTE: the exact 6-step ordinal is a pedagogical representation; the peer-reviewed literature
firmly supports "maintenance first, production (growth then eggs) last" and that thermoregulation and
feather condition are major maintenance costs, but does not publish this precise ranking as such. The
course presents it honestly as a teaching order, not a proven ordinal.

**Acute phase protein link ([A4] + [A1] Oke 2024):** oxidative stress / immune activation switches on
the acute phase response. The liver ramps up costly acute phase protein synthesis using amino acids
mobilized from muscle, and the bird eats less (anorexia). This demand "jumps the queue" at the top of
the energy ladder, so growth and eggs (the bottom) are cut first, before visible disease.
